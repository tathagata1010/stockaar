If you're standing at the edge of the Indian stock market wondering where to begin, you're in good company. Over **15 crore** demat accounts have been opened across NSDL and CDSL — more than half of them in just the last four years. Retail participation has exploded, and the tools have never been more accessible. This guide walks you through everything you need to start, from picking a broker to placing your first informed trade.

## What you actually need to start

The Indian stock market is regulated by **SEBI** (Securities and Exchange Board of India). To buy or sell any listed share, you need three accounts wired together:

1. **A bank account** — for moving money in and out.
2. **A trading account** — opened with a SEBI-registered broker (Zerodha, Groww, Upstox, ICICI Direct, etc.).
3. **A demat account** — held with a depository participant (DP), where your shares are stored electronically.

Most brokers bundle the trading and demat accounts together, and onboarding is now fully digital. You'll need your PAN, Aadhaar, a cancelled cheque or bank statement, and a selfie. The whole KYC process typically takes 24–48 hours.

## NSE vs BSE: what's the difference?

India has two main stock exchanges:

- **NSE (National Stock Exchange)** — newer, electronic from day one, accounts for ~90% of daily equity volume. Home of the Nifty 50 index.
- **BSE (Bombay Stock Exchange)** — Asia's oldest exchange, founded in 1875. Home of the Sensex.

Most large companies are listed on **both** exchanges. When you place a buy order, your broker routes it to whichever exchange offers the better price. As a beginner, you can mostly ignore the distinction and trade through NSE.

## Market timings (IST)

- **Pre-open**: 9:00 – 9:15 (price discovery only)
- **Continuous trading**: 9:15 – 15:30, Monday to Friday
- **Closing session**: 15:30 – 16:00

Markets are closed on weekends and on a published list of holidays each year (around 14 days). Some derivative segments stay open longer, but for equity delivery trades, the 9:15–15:30 window is what matters.

## The two ways to invest

**Delivery (CNC — Cash and Carry)** is when you buy shares with the intent to **hold** them. The shares hit your demat in T+1 working day. There's no time pressure — you can hold for 5 minutes or 50 years.

**Intraday (MIS — Margin Intraday Square-off)** is when you buy and sell the same security in the same trading session. Your broker offers leverage, but every open position is auto-closed before 3:20 PM. This is closer to trading than investing, and the data is clear: most retail intraday traders lose money. As a beginner, **stick to delivery**.

## Your first investment: keep it boring

A few sensible rules for your first ₹10,000:

1. **Buy what you know** — Reliance, HDFC Bank, TCS, ITC, Infosys. Familiar businesses you actually understand.
2. **Diversify across at least 5 stocks** so a single bad pick doesn't sink you.
3. **Spread purchases over time** — split your ₹10K across 2–3 months rather than dumping it on day one.
4. **Reinvest dividends** — they compound into surprisingly large numbers over a decade.

You don't need to swing for the fences. Compounding at 12–14% annually (the long-term Nifty average) doubles your money every ~5–6 years.

## Taxes you should know

- **Short-term capital gains** (held less than 12 months): 20% flat.
- **Long-term capital gains** (held over 12 months): 12.5% on gains above ₹1.25 lakh per financial year.
- **STT (Securities Transaction Tax)** is auto-deducted by your broker on every trade.
- **Dividends** are taxed at your slab rate.

Keep a contract note folder or use a portfolio tracker — come tax season you'll thank yourself.

## What to avoid in your first year

- **Penny stocks under ₹10** — most are illiquid or manipulated.
- **F&O (futures & options)** — looks attractive, statistically destroys 9 out of 10 retail traders (SEBI's own data).
- **Tips from WhatsApp / Telegram groups** — almost always pump-and-dump schemes.
- **Margin trading** — borrowing money to invest amplifies losses just as fast as gains.

## Where to go from here

Once you're comfortable placing orders, learn how to:

- Read a company's annual report (start with the chairman's letter).
- Use a stock screener to filter by valuation and quality.
- Build a simple SIP into an index fund alongside your stock picks.

The Indian stock market rewards patience more than cleverness. The investors who do well aren't the ones reading the most charts — they're the ones who buy good businesses and hold them long enough for the math to work.
