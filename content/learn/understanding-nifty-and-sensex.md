Open any financial newspaper and the first two numbers you'll see are the **Sensex** and the **Nifty 50**. They're the heartbeat of India's stock market — but most retail investors don't really know what they represent. Here's the short, clear explanation.

## What is an index?

An **index** is a calculated number that tracks the combined value of a chosen basket of stocks. When the index goes up, it means the overall basket is worth more. It's a single number that summarises the mood of the entire market — or at least the slice of it you're tracking.

You can't actually "buy" an index, but you can buy **index funds** and **ETFs** that mirror them.

## The Sensex (S&P BSE Sensex)

- **Run by**: BSE (in collaboration with S&P Dow Jones).
- **Number of stocks**: 30 large-cap Indian companies.
- **Launched**: 1986, with a base value of 100 (set to 1979 levels).
- **Methodology**: Free-float market capitalisation weighted.

"Free-float" means only the shares actually available for public trading count — not those locked up with promoters, the government, or strategic holders. So a company where promoters own 70% will have a smaller weight in the index than its raw market cap suggests.

Today's Sensex constituents include the usual suspects: Reliance Industries, HDFC Bank, TCS, ICICI Bank, Infosys, ITC, L&T, and so on. It gets rebalanced periodically to keep it representative.

## The Nifty 50

- **Run by**: NSE Indices (a subsidiary of NSE).
- **Number of stocks**: 50 large-cap companies.
- **Launched**: 1996, with a base value of 1,000 (set to 1995 levels).
- **Methodology**: Also free-float market cap weighted.

The Nifty 50 captures a broader slice than the Sensex — 50 stocks instead of 30 — and is the more widely tracked index among institutional investors, derivatives traders and global fund managers.

## Why two indices?

Historical accident, mostly. BSE created the Sensex first because BSE was older. When NSE launched in the 1990s with better technology, it built its own flagship index. Today both exchanges trade essentially the same stocks, but the two indices remain because trillions of rupees of investment products are benchmarked against them.

## How the numbers are calculated

The formula (simplified) for both indices is:

```
Index value = (Current free-float market cap of basket / Base market cap) × Base index value
```

So when you see "Nifty up 1.2%", it means the **weighted average price** of the basket rose 1.2% versus the previous close. Big stocks move the index more than small ones. A 5% move in Reliance (Nifty weight: ~10%) will shift the index more than a 5% move in a smaller constituent.

## Why does the index move?

Three forces, roughly in order of impact:

1. **Earnings** — when companies in the basket report better profits, prices rise, index rises.
2. **Liquidity** — when FIIs (foreign institutional investors) pour money into Indian equities, demand pushes prices up.
3. **Macro / news** — RBI rate decisions, GDP prints, election results, global shocks, oil prices, US Fed moves — all feed into sentiment.

On any given day, indices can move 1–2% on pure sentiment. Over years, earnings dominate. Long-term, the Nifty has compounded at roughly **12–14% per year** (including dividends).

## Other Indian indices worth knowing

- **Bank Nifty** — 12 largest banking stocks. Most heavily traded derivative in India.
- **Nifty Next 50** — the 50 stocks ranked 51–100, the "future Nifty" candidates.
- **Nifty Midcap 150** and **Nifty Smallcap 250** — broader, more volatile.
- **Sectoral indices** — Nifty IT, Nifty Pharma, Nifty FMCG, Nifty Auto, etc.

## How to actually invest in an index

You don't need to buy 50 individual stocks. The cheapest options are:

- **Index funds** (mutual fund route, e.g. UTI Nifty 50 Index Fund, HDFC Nifty 50 Index Fund). Buy via SIP, fees ~0.1–0.3% annually.
- **ETFs** (exchange-traded, e.g. Nippon ETF Nifty BeES, SBI ETF Sensex). Buy like a stock on NSE/BSE, slightly cheaper.

Both deliver returns matching the underlying index minus a tiny tracking error. For most retail investors, **a Nifty 50 SIP is the best default portfolio core** — beats 80%+ of active fund managers over 10-year periods.

## What the index doesn't tell you

The Sensex hitting an all-time high doesn't mean **your stocks** are up. If you're holding mid-caps, small-caps, or specific sectors, your portfolio can lag for months even when the index roars. Always look at your portfolio's actual returns, not the headline number on TV.
