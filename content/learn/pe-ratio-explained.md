If you've spent more than ten minutes around stock market commentary, you've heard the phrase "this stock is trading at a P/E of 22" — usually delivered with great authority. Let's unpack what that actually means, when it's useful, and the situations where it can lead you badly astray.

## The definition

**P/E** stands for **Price-to-Earnings ratio**. It's the simplest possible valuation calculation:

```
P/E = Share price / Earnings per share (EPS)
```

If a stock trades at ₹1,000 and the company earned ₹50 per share over the past 12 months, the P/E is **20**. You're paying ₹20 today for every ₹1 of annual profit.

Equivalently, if earnings stay flat forever, it would take you 20 years to "earn back" what you paid. (They never stay flat — which is exactly the point.)

## Trailing vs Forward P/E

- **Trailing P/E (TTM)**: uses the last 12 months of actual reported earnings. Solid, but backward-looking.
- **Forward P/E**: uses analyst-estimated earnings for the next 12 months. More relevant to investing decisions, but estimates can be wrong.

When financial media just say "P/E", they usually mean trailing. Always check which is being quoted.

## What's "expensive" or "cheap"?

There's no universal answer. Context is everything.

| Sector | Typical Nifty P/E range |
|---|---|
| Banks | 12–22 |
| IT services | 22–32 |
| FMCG / consumer | 40–80 |
| Auto | 15–30 |
| Power utilities | 10–18 |
| Pharma | 25–40 |
| Real estate | 15–30 |

A 50 P/E is wildly cheap for Nestle India (which trades at 70+) but obscenely expensive for Coal India (which trades at 7). **Always compare a stock's P/E to its sector and its own history**, not to a magic number.

The Nifty 50 itself usually trades between **18–28**. Above 28 historically means "expensive" — the market is paying a premium for the same earnings.

## Why high P/E ≠ bad

Investors are willing to pay more (a higher P/E) when:

- **Earnings are growing fast** — a company growing 25% a year deserves a higher multiple than one growing 5%.
- **Quality is exceptional** — high return on equity, low debt, durable competitive moat.
- **The sector is structurally favoured** — IT services in early 2000s, EV makers today.

This is the entire idea behind **PEG ratio** (Price/Earnings to Growth):

```
PEG = P/E / Earnings growth rate (%)
```

A PEG below 1 is generally considered attractive. Asian Paints at P/E 70 with 15% growth has PEG ~4.7 (rich). HDFC Bank at P/E 20 with 18% growth has PEG ~1.1 (reasonable).

## Why low P/E ≠ good

A 5 P/E sounds like a bargain. It often isn't. Common reasons stocks trade at very low P/E:

1. **Earnings are about to collapse** — the market knows the next quarter is going to be ugly.
2. **The business is in structural decline** — landlines, paging, fossil-fuel-heavy utilities, traditional media.
3. **Governance concerns** — promoter pledging, accounting irregularities, related-party transactions.
4. **High debt / interest coverage risk** — bankruptcy is a real possibility.
5. **Cyclical peak** — commodity producers often look cheapest right at the top of a cycle.

This is the famous **value trap**. Yes Bank traded at single-digit P/E for years before its 2020 implosion. So did many PSU stocks for the better part of a decade.

## When P/E doesn't work at all

Don't use P/E for:

- **Loss-making companies** — denominator is negative, ratio is meaningless. (Many new-age tech IPOs.)
- **Cyclicals at the peak/trough of cycles** — use P/B or EV/EBITDA instead.
- **Banks** — better metrics are P/B and price-to-pre-provision-operating-profit.
- **REITs / InvITs** — use price-to-distributions or NAV-based valuation.
- **Holding companies** — sum-of-parts is more honest.

## A practical checklist before trusting a P/E

1. Is the company **profitable** on a sustained basis? One-off gains can flatter the denominator.
2. Are the earnings of **decent quality**? (Cash flow ≈ accounting profit?)
3. What's the **5-year median P/E** for the stock? Is today's number above or below it?
4. What's the **sector median P/E**?
5. What's the **earnings growth outlook**? (PEG)
6. Any **governance / debt red flags**?

If a stock answers all six honestly, then and only then is a low P/E a real value signal.

## The takeaway

P/E is the most useful single-number screener in your toolkit — and the most overused. Treat it as a **question**, not an **answer**: "Why is this stock at this P/E?" The companies that compound shareholder wealth over decades almost never look like the cheapest names on a P/E screen. They look reasonable, deserve their multiple, and just keep growing.
