If you could only learn to read **one** financial statement, the **balance sheet** would be the right one. The income statement tells you what a company **earned**; the balance sheet tells you what it **is**. It's the financial photograph of a business at a single moment in time.

This guide walks you through how to read a balance sheet of any Indian listed company in under 10 minutes — and what to actually look for.

## The fundamental equation

Every balance sheet, anywhere in the world, balances:

```
Assets = Liabilities + Shareholders' Equity
```

What the company **owns** (assets) is funded by what it **owes** (liabilities) plus the money put in by **owners** (equity). That's it. Everything else is detail.

## Where to find it

In an Indian company's annual report (downloadable from BSE/NSE or the company's investor relations page), the balance sheet usually appears around page 100–150 under "**Standalone**" and "**Consolidated**" sections. **Always read the consolidated version** — it reflects subsidiaries too.

## Assets — what the company owns

Assets are split into **Non-current** (held long term) and **Current** (turned into cash within 12 months).

### Non-current assets

- **Property, Plant and Equipment (PPE)** — factories, machinery, buildings. Big number for manufacturers (Tata Steel, Reliance), tiny for asset-light businesses (Infosys, HDFC AMC).
- **Goodwill and intangibles** — premium paid in acquisitions, software, patents. Big goodwill = lots of M&A; quality of acquisitions matters.
- **Investments** — long-term stakes in other companies, mutual funds, bonds.
- **Right-of-use assets** — capitalised lease obligations (post-Ind AS 116).

### Current assets

- **Inventories** — raw materials, work-in-progress, finished goods. Watch for sudden buildup (could signal slowing demand).
- **Trade receivables** — money customers owe but haven't paid yet. Should grow roughly in line with revenue. Faster growth = collection problems.
- **Cash and cash equivalents** — actual liquid cash.
- **Short-term investments** — fixed deposits, liquid funds.

## Liabilities — what the company owes

Same split: **Non-current** (due after 12 months) and **Current** (due within 12 months).

### Non-current liabilities

- **Long-term borrowings** — bank loans, debentures, term loans.
- **Deferred tax liabilities** — timing differences in tax accounting.
- **Lease liabilities (non-current)** — long-term lease obligations.

### Current liabilities

- **Short-term borrowings** — working capital loans, cash credit.
- **Trade payables** — money owed to suppliers.
- **Current maturities of long-term debt** — portion of long loans due in next 12 months.
- **Other current liabilities** — accrued expenses, GST payable, etc.

## Shareholders' equity — what owners "own"

- **Equity share capital** — face value × number of shares.
- **Reserves and surplus** — accumulated retained profits + share premium + other reserves. **This is usually the biggest number in the equity section** for a mature business.
- **Other equity** — typically grouped with reserves.

A company that has been profitable for decades has a huge **reserves** number; that's the compounding of retained earnings.

## The five ratios you should compute in your head

### 1. Debt-to-equity

```
D/E = Total Debt / Total Equity
```

Anything under **0.5** is conservative. Above **1.5** is concerning for non-financial companies. **Always 0 for IT services** companies (TCS, Infosys), naturally high for **banks and NBFCs** — compare within sector.

### 2. Current ratio

```
Current ratio = Current assets / Current liabilities
```

Should be > **1.0** at minimum. **1.5–2.0** is healthy. Below 1.0 means short-term liquidity stress.

### 3. Cash conversion cycle (CCC)

For businesses you can compute it from balance sheet plus income statement:

```
CCC = Days Inventory + Days Receivables – Days Payables
```

Lower is better. Negative is exceptional (you collect before you pay — Hindustan Unilever, ITC, Dmart all have this).

### 4. Book value per share

```
BV per share = Shareholders' Equity / Shares outstanding
```

P/B = Price / BV per share. Useful for **banks, NBFCs, real estate** where assets dominate.

### 5. Return on Equity (ROE)

```
ROE = Net Profit / Shareholders' Equity
```

A consistent ROE above **15%** is the hallmark of a quality compounder. India's best businesses (Asian Paints, Nestle India, HDFC Bank historically) have averaged 20%+ ROE for two decades.

## Red flags to scan for

1. **Rising debt with falling profits.** The classic prelude to a downgrade or default.
2. **Receivables growing faster than revenue.** Suggests customers aren't paying — or the company is stuffing the channel.
3. **Goodwill > 25% of total assets.** Lots of overpaid acquisitions, future write-downs possible.
4. **Cash declining quarter over quarter while reported profits rise.** Possible accounting aggression — earnings aren't backed by cash.
5. **Contingent liabilities** (in the notes) growing — pending lawsuits, tax disputes, guarantees given.
6. **Promoter pledge** (separate filing) above 30%. Promoters have borrowed against their shares; a price drop forces a sale.

## A real example: how to skim TCS

Open TCS's annual report and you'll find:

- **Cash + investments**: massive — TCS holds tens of thousands of crores in cash.
- **Debt**: effectively zero. D/E = 0.
- **Reserves**: enormous — decades of retained profits.
- **Property + equipment**: relatively small for the company's revenue (services business, not manufacturing).
- **Goodwill**: small — TCS grows organically, doesn't acquire much.

This is the picture of a high-quality compounder. Compare it to a heavily-leveraged real estate developer and the contrast will be obvious in 60 seconds.

## The two-minute scan

When opening any balance sheet for the first time:

1. **Is debt growing or shrinking** over the last 3 years?
2. **Is equity (reserves) compounding** at a reasonable rate?
3. **Are receivables growing in line with revenue**?
4. **Is cash growing**?
5. **Any large goodwill or intangibles that look suspect**?

If the answer to 1 is "shrinking", 2 is "compounding nicely", 3 is "in line", 4 is "yes", and 5 is "no" — you're probably looking at a quality business. From there, you dive deeper.
