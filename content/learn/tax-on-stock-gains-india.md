Tax on equity gains is one of the most-Googled investing topics in India — and for good reason. The rules changed materially in the 2024 Union Budget, and most blog posts you'll find are still outdated. This guide reflects the **rules currently in force for FY 2025–26 onwards**.

## The two flavours of capital gains

When you sell an equity share (or an equity mutual fund), the difference between your sale price and your cost is a **capital gain** (or loss). How it's taxed depends on **how long you held it**.

- **Short-Term Capital Gain (STCG)** — held for **12 months or less**.
- **Long-Term Capital Gain (LTCG)** — held for **more than 12 months**.

(For unlisted shares, debt funds, gold, and real estate the holding-period thresholds and rates are different. This article is about listed equity only.)

## STCG: 20% flat

Short-term gains on listed equity shares and equity-oriented mutual funds are taxed at a **flat 20%**, irrespective of your income slab.

**Worked example.** You bought 100 shares of Infosys at ₹1,600 each on 5 January 2026 (cost ₹1,60,000). You sold them at ₹1,850 on 10 August 2026 (proceeds ₹1,85,000). Holding period: ~7 months → STCG.

- Gain = ₹25,000
- Tax = 20% × ₹25,000 = **₹5,000**

This is in addition to STT and brokerage, which are deducted at trade time and **not separately deductible** for income-tax purposes.

## LTCG: 12.5% above ₹1.25 lakh

Long-term gains are taxed at **12.5%**, but the **first ₹1.25 lakh of LTCG per financial year is exempt**.

**Worked example.** Across FY 2026–27 you booked LTCG of ₹3,00,000 across all your equity sales.

- Exempt portion = ₹1,25,000
- Taxable LTCG = ₹1,75,000
- Tax = 12.5% × ₹1,75,000 = **₹21,875**

The ₹1.25 lakh exemption is a per-year, per-person threshold — it doesn't carry forward. **Many savvy investors deliberately sell winning long-term positions every March to "use up" the exemption** (a strategy called *tax-loss harvesting* or, when used to crystallise gains, *tax-gain harvesting*).

## Grandfathering for pre-2018 buys

If you bought shares **before 1 February 2018**, gains accrued up to **31 January 2018** are grandfathered (not taxed). Your cost basis becomes the higher of:

- Actual purchase price, or
- The closing price on 31 January 2018 (capped at the eventual sale price).

This shrinks taxable LTCG considerably for very old holdings. Most brokers apply this automatically in their tax P&L reports.

## STT — already deducted

**Securities Transaction Tax (STT)** is auto-collected by your broker on every equity trade:

- Delivery sell (CNC): 0.10% of trade value (both buy and sell legs).
- Intraday sell: 0.025% on sell side.
- F&O sell: lower rates, similar structure.

STT is **not deductible** from your capital gains — think of it as the cost of using a regulated exchange.

## Dividends — taxed at slab rate

Since FY 2020–21, dividend distribution tax was scrapped. **Dividends are now taxed in your hands at your applicable income-tax slab.**

- If you earn ₹20 lakh salary (30% slab) and receive ₹50,000 dividends, you'll owe roughly ₹15,000 + cess on those dividends.
- The company will TDS at 10% on dividends above ₹5,000 in a financial year. You can claim this back if your overall slab is lower or pay the differential if higher.

This is why high-dividend stocks aren't always as attractive as they look — buybacks and growth stocks can be more tax-efficient for high-income investors.

## Losses — what you can do with them

The Income Tax Act allows **losses to be set off against gains**, with some restrictions:

- **STCL (short-term capital loss)** — can be set off against **both STCG and LTCG** in the same year.
- **LTCL (long-term capital loss)** — can be set off **only against LTCG**.
- **Unutilised capital losses** can be **carried forward for 8 years**, but only if you **file your ITR on or before the due date**.

This makes it strategically smart to **book paper losses near year-end** to offset realised gains. Just remember the *wash sale* style rule: if you sell at a loss and re-buy the same security shortly after, the Assessing Officer can question intent.

## Which ITR form do you file?

- **ITR-1**: only if you have no capital gains. Most stock investors don't qualify.
- **ITR-2**: salaried / pensioners with capital gains. **This is what most retail equity investors use.**
- **ITR-3**: if you're treating trading as a business (intraday/F&O at large volumes).
- **ITR-4**: for presumptive business / professional income — generally not relevant to stock investors.

Brokers like Zerodha, Upstox, ICICI Direct provide a downloadable **tax P&L report** — feed it to ClearTax, Quicko or your CA and you're done.

## Intraday and F&O are taxed differently

These are **not capital gains**.

- **Intraday equity** = speculative business income. Taxed at your slab rate, losses can only be set off against speculative gains.
- **F&O (futures and options)** = non-speculative business income. Taxed at slab rate, losses can be set off against any business income except salary.

This is why filing for active traders gets complicated quickly — and why tax audits are often required if turnover crosses certain thresholds.

## Three pieces of practical advice

1. **Hold for at least 12 months and 1 day.** The jump from 20% STCG to 12.5% LTCG is enormous on large positions.
2. **Realise ₹1.25 lakh of LTCG every March.** Sell a long-held winner, immediately buy back — your cost basis resets higher, tax-free.
3. **Keep your contract notes for 7 years.** That's the lookback period the IT department can ask you for documentation.

When in doubt — especially with F&O, ESOP, or NRI tax issues — pay a CA. The fee is a fraction of one mis-filing.
